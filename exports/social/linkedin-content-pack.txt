Post 1 of 3: Product Launch (Primary)

Headline: I built a daily intelligence pipeline that runs itself — here's what I learned

Body:

Six months ago, I started building something that felt impossible: a fully autonomous daily intelligence briefing that produces professional-grade assessments every morning at 07:00 PT.

Not a ChatGPT wrapper. Not a newsletter with automated summarization. A structured analytic pipeline.

Here's what actually happens between midnight and 7am:

1. Collection (30 min)
Fifteen durable OSINT sources pulled simultaneously — ISW Publications, CTP/AEI, Reuters, AP, BBC, Al Jazeera, AFP, wire feeds. Every incident tagged with NATO Admiralty reliability/credibility codes at the point of capture. Not retroactively — at capture.

2. Analysis (90 min)
DeepSeek V4 Pro analyzes each of six theatres independently. Every judgment gets a Sherman-Kent verbal band AND a numeric 7-day prediction percentage. If the model returns everything at 80-95% confidence, that gets flagged as a calibration smell — over-confidence is the most common failure mode of LLM analysis, and the pipeline is designed to catch it.

3. Quality gates (30 min)
Five-stage validation: calibration consistency, source hygiene, red-team pass, structural completeness, visual integrity. If a gate fails, the pipeline stops — no degraded product ships silently.

4. Production (60 min)
Maps, charts, Mermaid diagrams, and final PDF assembly. Three editions: Regular (full Sherman-Kent, 43pp), Tactical (ThruDark visual language), Magazine (Economist-inspired editorial design).

5. Delivery
Lands in the subscriber's inbox at ~07:00 PT.

The result is the Global Security & Intelligence Brief — a daily product that treats intelligence as a repeatable methodology, not an opinion page.

Why this matters: Most "AI intelligence" products are summarization engines. This one is an analytic pipeline with structured tradecraft, source grading, calibration enforcement, and quality gates. It doesn't tell you what it thinks — it tells you what it knows, how confident it is, and what would change its mind.

If you work in geopolitical risk, security analysis, or strategic intelligence, I'd love to hear your take on the methodology.

Post 2 of 3: Methodology Deep-Dive (Thought Leadership)

Headline: Why "I read 50 sources and here's what I think" is not intelligence

Body:

In the last three months I've tested dozens of AI "intelligence" products. Almost all of them follow the same pattern:

1. Scrape headlines
2. Feed to GPT
3. Output a summary

That's not intelligence. That's a reading comprehension exercise with better formatting.

Real intelligence analysis requires:

• Source grading before analysis — not after
Every incident in our daily brief is tagged with a NATO Admiralty code at the point of capture. The analyst never sees an ungraded source. This prevents the model from treating a regime-aligned outlet the same as a wire service.

• Calibrated judgment bands
"Likely" doesn't mean "probably." In Sherman-Kent methodology, "likely" means 55-70%. "Highly likely" means 75-85%. If you're publishing assessments without numeric anchors, you're not making predictions — you're expressing opinions.

• Alternative analysis baked in
Every edition forces a red-team pass on at least one key judgment. If the evidence supports Hypothesis A, the pipeline explicitly asks: "What would need to be true for Hypothesis B to be correct?" This is basic structured analytic technique that most automated products skip entirely.

• Transparency about gaps
If a region has no usable reporting in a 24-hour window, we say so explicitly. Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, and we flag collection gaps rather than pretending coverage is comprehensive.

The Global Security & Intelligence Brief applies these techniques at daily cadence. Six theatres. Structured methodology. Delivered at 07:00 PT.

I'm not claiming it replaces a human analyst. I'm claiming it's the most rigorous daily product that can be fully automated — and that the methodology matters more than the model.

Post 3 of 3: Prediction Markets Angle (Trading/Finance Audience)

Headline: I added prediction markets to an intelligence brief — here's why it works

Body:

Every edition of the Global Security & Intelligence Brief ends with the same section: ten Polymarket trades sized for a balanced book, with entry prices, edge, and a regional read-through.

Why prediction markets belong in an intelligence product:

1. They force precision
When you say "the ceasefire is fragile," you can be right or wrong and nobody remembers. When you say "I'll short Russia-Ukraine ceasefire at current prices across JUN26 and SEP26 tenors," you have a timestamped, falsifiable prediction. The market settles it.

2. They expose divergence
The most valuable signal in the brief is where market consensus diverges from front-line collection. When Polymarket prices Ukraine ceasefire at 45¢ but your on-the-ground sources suggest something different — that's an edge worth surfacing.

3. They create accountability
Every edition's prediction market section is archived. You can go back and check which calls worked and which didn't. That's a feedback loop most intelligence products deliberately avoid.

Recent calls (3 May edition):
• Short Russia-Ukraine ceasefire — both JUN26 and SEP26 tenors
• Short US-Iran deal — both tenors
• Long India-Pakistan exchange tail at 28¢
• Fade Maduro exit at 64¢

Each backed by region-level structured analysis, not vibes.

The prediction market section isn't a gimmick. It's a calibration tool — for the pipeline and for the reader.
