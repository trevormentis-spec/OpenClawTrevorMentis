# Framework: Cartel Factional Dynamics

**Purpose:** Track and forecast cartel factional competition across Mexico
**Scope:** Intra-faction wars (CDS), inter-cartel contests (CJNG-CU), peripheral posture (CDN)
**Method:** 6-axis scoring evaluated weekly against three active fronts
**Generalizability note:** This framework is designed to redirect at any criminal-conflict geography (Balkans OC, Tri-Border Area, Sahel trafficking routes) with no structural modification. The axis definitions are criminal-faction-universal. Mexico-specific content goes in the knowledge files, not the framework schema.

---

## The 6 Axes

### A1 — Territory: Current geographic footprint, expansion front, contested zones
- **What to assess:** Which municipalities/states does the faction control, contest, or compete in
- **Scale:** Core (stable control) → Contested (active conflict) → Peripheral (limited presence) → None
- **Signal:** Narcomantas claiming territory, SEDENA deployment patterns, local government collusion signals, body dump locations
- **Confidence note:** Government control maps (SEDENA/SESNSP) differ significantly from Lantia/InSight Crime ground truth. Cross-reference both.

### A2 — Revenue Streams: Primary and secondary income sources, resilience to interdiction
- **What to assess:** Which illicit markets dominate revenue: fentanyl export, meth production, heroin/opium, cocaine transit, KFR, extortion (piso), fuel theft (huachicoleo), mining theft, avocado/lemon extortion, migrant smuggling
- **Scale:** Dominant (>50% revenue) → Significant (25-50%) → Secondary (10-25%) → Minor (<10%)
- **Signal:** Seizure composition changes (DEA/CBP data), cargo theft patterns by region, extortion rate changes in specific industries, huachicoleo pipeline targeting changes
- **Note:** Revenue stream assessment is the lowest-confidence axis — good data is scarce. Use interdiction data as proxy, not ground truth.

### A3 — Succession State: Leadership structure stability and transition proximity
- **What to assess:** Current leader status (health, incarceration, active), known or probable successors, succession mechanism (inherited, elected, violent), factional unity under leadership
- **Scale:** Stable (clear leadership, no visible challenge) → Fraying (internal tension, junior leader emerging) → Contested (active succession conflict) → Open (post-arrest/kill power vacuum)
- **Signal:** Captured leader clan reactions (vengeance vs accommodation), El Mencho health rumors, Chapitos-Mayos family dynamics, narcolists of police/military collusion

### A4 — Alliances: Active coalitions, truces, non-aggression pacts, state-embedded relationships
- **What to assess:** Formal/informal alliances with other factions, state-level government relationships, military/police penetration level, political corruption network reach
- **Scale:** Embedded (state-level government alliance) → Allied (formal pact with other cartel) → Neutral (non-conflict with adjacent groups) → Hostile (active conflict)
- **Signal:** SEDENA operations favoritism (targeting one faction over another), narcomanta claims of alliance, prison visits between faction leaders, police collusion patterns in specific jurisdictions
- **Note:** This axis interacts most with us_mexico theme (DEA/OFAC/FBI penetration of alliances)

### A5 — Operational Tempo: Volume and character of kinetic activity
- **What to assess:** Homicide rates by municipality (SESNSP), narco-blockade frequency, law enforcement targeting rate, high-visibility violence (decapitations, mass graves, public executions), military confrontation frequency
- **Scale:** High (daily kinetic activity across multiple states) → Moderate (periodic significant events) → Low (routine consolidation, no major ops) → Dormant (avoiding confrontation)
- **Signal:** Daily narco-bloqueo reports, police assassination rates, SESNSP homicides trend (monthly), military confrontations (SEDENA daily report), mass grave discovery frequency

### A6 — State Penetration: Depth of corruption within government institutions
- **What to assess:** Local police collusion, state government relationships, federal-level penetration (judiciary, SEDENA, FGR), campaign finance infiltration, intelligence service compromise
- **Scale:** Captured (institution operates for cartel interests) → Deeply penetrated (cartel influence operational) → Moderate (limited corruption, some resistance) → Incorrupt (effective institutional integrity)
- **Signal:** Local police chief assassination rates (anti-corruption signal), court ruling patterns on cartel cases, FGR investigation targeting (which cartels get pursued), state-level official arrests, narco-political scandals (Rocha case)

## Current Front Assessments (2026-05-15) — CORRECTED

### Front 1 — Sinaloa Intra-CDS War: Los Chapitos vs Los Mayos

**CRITICAL UPDATE:** As of May 2026, the Sinaloa leadership situation has transformed:
- **El Mayo Zambada:** Captured July 25, 2024 (lured onto plane by Joaquín Guzmán López). Pleaded guilty Aug 2025. Sentenced life Jan 2026.
- **Mayos faction now leaderless** at the top. Ismael Zambada Imperial ("El Mayito Gordo") likely leading, but his capture history and cooperation legacy weaken his authority.
- **Chapitos:** Iván Archivaldo Guzmán Salazar is the primary remaining leader at large. Brothers Ovidio and Joaquín in US custody. Jesús Alfredo status uncertain. Iván is reportedly seeking a deal with US authorities (LA Times, May 12, 2026).
- **Rocha indictment:** US charges against Sinaloa Governor Rocha (Apr 29) names Iván Archivaldo as ordering violence in 2021 election. This is the highest-signal US action against Chapitos to date.

| Axis | Chapitos | Mayos | Assessment |
|------|----------|-------|------------|
| **A1 Territory** | Contested. Core: Culiacán, Los Mochis. Losing ground in Sierra Madre | Contested but retreating. Core: Mazatlán, Sierra foothills. Mayos losing Durango-Chihuahua corridor due to leadership vacuum | Territorial dynamic shifting — Chapitos consolidating where Mayos are in leadership transition. Monitor Durango highway for narcobloqueo frequency as indicator of contested corridor control |
| **A2 Revenue** | Dominant: Fentanyl (primary). Secondary: Meth | Significant: Opium/heroin (traditional). Growing: Fentanyl via alliance with Beltran-Leyva remnants | Revenue diverging — Chapitos invested heavily in fentanyl (high risk/high reward), Mayos retain traditional heroin (stable declines). Succession conflict may be partly revenue-structure driven |
| **A3 Succession** | Contested. Following Ivan Guzman's arrest, internal Chapitos leadership uncertain | Stable. El Mayo (80+) succession approaching but known internal candidates | Chapitos succession uncertainty is the primary driver — if they consolidate under a single leader, expect operational tempo increase vs Mayos |
| **A4 Alliances** | Isolated. Enemies with Mayos, CDN, CJNG | Moderate. Potential informal truce with CJNG via Beltran-Leyva bridge | Chapitos' multi-directional hostility is unsustainable long-term. Expect either alliance-building or territorial contraction |
| **A5 Tempo** | High. Daily Culiacán kinetic activity. Highway blockades, military confrontations | Moderate. Lower visibility, more strategic targeting | Chapitos tempo is attritional and self-damaging. Mayos tempo suggests consolidation posture. If Chapitos over-commit, Mayos counter-escalate |
| **A6 State Penetration** | Significant. Rocha case signals deep Sinaloa state penetration by CDS broadly | Uncertain. Less directly implicated in recent scandals | Rocha case is the highest-signal state penetration indicator available. Monitor whether investigation targets Chapitos-linked officials specifically |

**Summary:** Intra-CDS war is in a contested plateau phase. Chapitos are more visible, more kinetic, and more vulnerable due to multi-directional hostility and succession uncertainty. Mayos are consolidating and waiting. The next decisive move may come from external pressure (US FTO designations, CIA Ground Branch) rather than internal CDS dynamics.

**Most likely trajectory (60%):** Chapitos fragment, with some operators going independent or joining Mayos within 12 months. A less likely but higher-impact scenario: a Chapo figure reconsolidates Chapitos under single leadership, restoring operational coherence.

### Front 2 — CJNG vs Cárteles Unidos (Michoacán) — CORRECTED

**CRITICAL UPDATE:** El Mencho was killed by Mexican Army on Feb 22, 2026 (operation led by Harfuch). Juan Carlos Valencia González ("El Pelón") confirmed as CJNG successor Apr 6, 2026. CJNG heavyweights El 85 and El Cuini now cooperating with US justice.

| Axis | CJNG (Post-Mencho) | Cárteles Unidos | Assessment |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|------------|
| **A1 Territory** | Uncertain. Core: Jalisco, Colima, Guanajuato. Post-Mencho territorial strategy still consolidating. Losing some CU-contested ground in Michoacán where succession uncertainty creates operational gap | Defensive but probing. Core: Michoacán (Tepalcatepec, Tierra Caliente). Sensing CJNG transition weakness | CJNG territorial momentum has paused in the post-Mencho transition. CU is defensive-consolidated but testing CJNG commitment to Michoacán expansion under new leadership |
| **A2 Revenue** | Dominant: Meth. Significant: Fentanyl, avocado extortion, mining theft, fuel theft. Revenue stream stable — operations self-sustaining at middle management level | Secondary: Extortion, local meth, mining theft, some fuel theft. Revenue insufficient for offensive operations | CJNG revenue system is decentralized enough to survive leadership transition. Revenue is not a short-term vulnerability |
| **A3 Succession** | **Transformed — post-founder.** El Mencho killed Feb 22. El Pelón (stepson, military commander) confirmed leader. El 85 and El Cuini cooperating with US — deep institutional knowledge loss. Leadership legitimacy untested | Fragmented. CU is a coalition of convenience. Multiple leaders. No single point of failure but no unified command | **This axis has flipped.** CJNG's succession certainty was its vulnerability under El Mencho (departure would = power vacuum). Post-Mencho, El Pelón faces the challenge of holding the organization together without personal authority. CU has an opportunity window that will close as El Pelón consolidates |
| **A4 Alliances** | Hostile to most national cartels. Jalisco state penetration uncertain — El Mencho's death may weaken protective relationships | CU local alliances unchanged. Self-defense and community police relationships are their defining strength | CU's alliances are stable. CJNG's institutional relationships in Jalisco may degrade without El Mencho's personal relationships maintaining them |
| **A5 Tempo** | Post-Mencho defensive posture. fewer high-visibility operations as internal consolidation occurs. Retaliatory violence limited | Moderate. CU not strong enough to go on offense but capable of local counter-attacks | Tempo has shifted — CJNG is internally focused, CU is unwilling or unable to exploit. Expect lower overall tempo in Michoacán for 2-3 months |
| **A6 State Penetration** | Uncertain in transition. Jalisco state penetration was El Mencho's personal network. El Pelón's ability to maintain those relationships is the key question | Moderate in Michoacán. Local government relationships stable | CJNG's state penetration may be the single greatest loss from El Mencho's death. Personal relationships with Jalisco officials do not automatically transfer to a new leader. This is the highest-impact unknown |

**Summary:** The post-Mencho transition has reshaped every axis. CJNG is in a defensive consolidation phase — still the most powerful cartel, but operationally constrained by leadership transition. CU has the best opportunity they will ever have to retake Tierra Caliente territory, but they are structurally incapable of offensive warfare. **The next 3 months will determine whether CJNG remains Mexico's dominant cartel or fragments into regional successor groups.**

**Most likely trajectory (50%):** CJNG consolidates under El Pelón with some territorial loss in Michoacán but maintains national dominance. CU holds but does not advance. A significant minority outcome (30%): CJNG fragments regionally with Jalisco core holding but Michoacán/Colima lost to CU or new actors.

### Front 3 — CDN Posture (Tamaulipas)

| Axis | CDN (Northeast Cartel) |
|------|------------------------|
| **A1 Territory** | Stable/Defensive. Core: Nuevo Laredo, Reynosa, Tamaulipas border corridor. Contested by Golfo Cartel remnants and Zetas Vieja Escuela |
| **A2 Revenue** | Dominant: Migrant smuggling (migration surges under Trump). Significant: Border tolls, KFR, cocaine transit | CDN's revenue mix is uniquely dependent on border transit — vulnerable to US border policy changes but currently buoyed by migration flows |
| **A3 Succession** | Stable-fraying. Leadership not publicly contested but Treviño lineage dynamics create tension | Less visible succession dynamic than other fronts. CDN is more institutionalized under a single command structure |
| **A4 Alliances** | Hostile: Golfo, Zetas remnants. Limited: No major national alliances | Isolated but geographically defensible — the Tamaulipas border corridor is a natural monopoly |
| **A5 Tempo** | Moderate. Border city violence is localized rather than state-wide | Tamaulipas violence has declined from 2010-2014 peak but remains structurally embedded |
| **A6 State Penetration** | Deep in Tamaulipas. Police, customs, and local government compromised historically | Tamaulipas has generational narco-state penetration. Harder to dislodge than measure |

**Summary:** CDN is the least dynamic of the three fronts — a mature criminal franchise operating a stable border monopoly. The primary change factor is US border policy (migration volumes driving revenue) and the Trump administration's FTO-related interventions.

**Most likely trajectory (70%):** Status quo maintained. CDN avoids high-visibility escalation that would trigger US unilateral action while maximizing revenue from migration flows. US FTO/Kinetic action is the external shock risk.
